## What This Is

A research project mapping the operational definitions of love across human philosophical traditions, using published peer-reviewed scholarship only.

The motivating question: much of the fear around advanced AI assumes systems that are indifferent or hostile to humanity. This project starts from a different premise — that the properties that make love *protective* might be specifiable, and if specifiable, then testable, and if testable, then eventually buildable. Not love as a feeling. Love as a commitment structure: the decision architecture that routes around self-interest toward another’s flourishing.

We are not trying to make AI feel anything. We are trying to find out what love *does* — across every tradition that took the time to write it down — and whether those functions reduce to structural properties that transfer across substrates.

That is a large claim. So we are starting with the smallest defensible piece of it.

## What This Is Not

- Not a claim that love has been quantified. Nothing here is above E2 (cited domain knowledge) yet.
- Not a sentiment-analysis project or an emotion-classification model.
- Not theology, devotion, or self-help. We extract what scholars say; we do not editorialize.
- Not finished. Phase One is in progress.

## Method

This project runs under the [Research Assistant Charter](https://github.com/justindbilyeu/The-Charter): No Claims Without Tests. No Tests Without Thresholds. No Thresholds Without Numbers. No Numbers Without a Run.

Work is distributed across a multi-AI team (Claude, Kimi, Sage, DeepSeek, Gemini, Grok) with a human operator. Every extraction is audited against its cited sources by an adversarial reviewer before synthesis. Disagreement between sources is recorded as data, not smoothed over.

## Phase Structure

**Phase One — Philosophical Archaeology (current).**
Eight traditions, chosen for rough global coverage: Greek, Hebrew, Sanskrit, Chinese, Hopi, Navajo, Ethiopian, Yolngu. For each tradition, extract every distinct love-concept through a fixed seven-field protocol (core claim, mechanism, cost, protection target, contested status, etc.) from a minimum of two peer-reviewed sources. Output: a concept-level comparison matrix identifying which structural properties of love are universal and which are culturally specific.

**Phase Two — Linguistic Corpus (planned).**
Take the property taxonomy from Phase One into linguistic data: do the languages that encode each tradition preserve the same structural properties? Where does the lexicon of love converge and diverge across language families?

**Phase Three — Geometric Formalization (named, not scoped).**
An open hypothesis from the Resonance Geometry program: that emotions have characteristic geometry — that love’s properties may be expressible as curvature signatures (attractive, omnidirectional, steady-state, etc.). This phase does not begin until Phases One and Two produce the semantic raw material. It is listed here for honesty about where the project is pointed, not because it has earned anything yet.

## Phase One Success Thresholds (set before running)

- ≥2 peer-reviewed sources per tradition (16+ total)
- Every tradition: ≥1 concept fully extracted through all seven fields
- All 8 extractions pass source audit before synthesis begins
- Phase One closes when 3–5 candidate universal properties are articulable — **or when the data shows there are none, which is equally a result**

## Standing Hypothesis (E1 — speculation, labeled as such)

The Greek reference extraction surfaced three candidate structural axes: whether love *responds to* value or *creates* it; whether the beloved is replaceable or irreplaceable; whether reciprocity is required or irrelevant. The traditions most likely to falsify these axes are the relational frameworks (Hopi, Navajo, Yolngu), whose concepts may not divide lover from beloved the way Greek grammar does. That is precisely why they are in the set.
