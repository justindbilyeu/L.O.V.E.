## Extraction: Three concepts

### Concept 1: Love above sectarianism (Ḥatäta Zär’a Ya’ǝqob)

|Field                |Extraction                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Tradition**        |Ethiopian (Ge’ez written philosophy, 17th c.)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
|**Love concept**     |Love as trans-sectarian criterion. The Lee/Worku/Belcher edition characterizes the Hatatas’ program as placing “reason above belief, desire above asceticism, love above sectarianism, and the natural world above the human.” See Flag 2 on the underlying Ge’ez lexeme.                                                                                                                                                                                          |
|**Core claim**       |Love functions as a rational-ethical criterion that overrides religious-communal boundaries: where doctrines divide (Orthodox, Catholic, Muslim, Jewish — all critiqued by Zera Yacob), love of fellow humans is what survives rational scrutiny as binding.                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
|**Mechanism**        |Rational examination (ḥatäta = inquiry). Zera Yacob subjects every doctrinal claim to the criterion of reason; what passes is what harmonizes with the goodness of creation. Love emerges not as commanded sentiment but as what inquiry vindicates. The theodicy frame: God is characterized as loving creator, and human ethics derives from rational apprehension of that ordering rather than from any sect’s law.                                             |
|**Cost**             |Exile and isolation — biographically literal: the inquiry that elevates love above sect cost Zera Yacob his position and drove him to a cave during the Susenyos-era persecutions. Within the system: the cost is surrendering communal-doctrinal belonging as a source of moral authority.                                                                                                                                                                        |
|**Protection target**|Persons across sect boundaries; religious coexistence itself. The scholarship consistently frames the Hatata as a response to religious civil conflict.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
|**Contested?**       |**YES — at the level of the text itself.** See Flag 1: the authenticity controversy (Conti Rossini’s 1920 forgery thesis attributing the texts to Giusto d’Urbino) remains a live scholarly dispute, now the subject of a dedicated 2024 De Gruyter volume. Recent scholarship (Lee/Worku/Belcher; contributors to Cantor/Egid/Merawi) trends toward Ethiopian authenticity, but the dispute is unresolved and must travel with every claim sourced to the Hatatas.|

## Flags (G4 ambiguity log)

**FLAG 1 — The tradition’s anchor texts have a live authenticity dispute.**
This is unique among our eight traditions: the dispute is not about what love means in the texts but about *who wrote the texts*. Conti Rossini (1920) argued the Hatatas were forged by their 19th-c. “discoverer,” the Capuchin Giusto d’Urbino; the charge kept the texts in obscurity for decades and carried explicit colonial assumptions (“real philosophy was impossible in Ethiopia”). The 2013 Wion work and the 2024 Cantor/Egid/Merawi volume treat the question as genuinely open scholarship; the major recent translation treats the texts as Ethiopian. For our matrix: every Hatata-derived claim carries this asterisk. Note the dispute’s *shape* — it is partly an argument about whether Africa could produce rationalist philosophy, which is itself data about how this tradition has been received.

**FLAG 2 — Lexeme verification gap.**
The scholarship I accessed discusses “love” in English. I could not verify in this session which Ge’ez lexeme (fǝqr is the standard Ge’ez/Amharic love-term) underlies each instance, nor whether the Hatatas deploy one term or several. Phase Two (linguistic corpus) must resolve this before any cross-tradition lexical claim. Until then, this extraction’s concepts are *scholarship-level*, not lexeme-level.

**FLAG 3 — The Orthodox Täwaḥǝdo theological literature is nearly absent from accessible English peer review.**
Ethiopia’s dominant living love-tradition — 1,700 years of Orthodox theology, liturgy, and the Ge’ez patristic corpus — is underrepresented in accessible English-language peer-reviewed philosophy. Our extraction therefore over-weights the rationalist Hatata lineage relative to the tradition’s actual center of gravity. Documented absence, not filled by speculation. Grok’s parallel run should hunt specifically for Täwaḥǝdo theology scholarship.

**FLAG 4 — Cross-tradition lineage.**
Heavy and explicit: the Hatatas engage Orthodox Christianity, Catholicism, Islam, and Judaism directly; Zera Yacob’s theodicy inherits the Psalms’ God-as-loving-creator frame (Hebrew lane overlap); early commentators saw Islamic kalam influence in the cosmological argument. This tradition cannot be treated as isolated — it is a deliberate synthesis point. Coordinate with Sage’s Hebrew extraction and the Greek agapē lineage flag.

## E1 — HYPOTHESIS ONLY (not for matrix entry without adversarial review)

1. **A new axis candidate: love’s relationship to *inquiry*.** Every previously extracted tradition grounds love in relation (kinship, covenant, reciprocity) or in metaphysics (ascent, gift). The Hatata lineage is the first to ground love in *rational examination* — love is what survives scrutiny when sectarian commands don’t. If this holds, the matrix gains an epistemic axis: is love prior to reason, opposed to reason, or vindicated by reason?
1. **Convergence note with Greek agapē, divergence in mechanism:** trans-boundary, person-regardless love appears in both, but agapē’s mechanism is unmotivated gift while the Hatata’s is rational vindication. Same protective shape, different engine — exactly the kind of structure/mechanism dissociation the project exists to find.
1. **The heart-integration claim (Concept 3) is the only extracted instance of a tradition explicitly *refusing* the reason/feeling split.** Worth tracking whether Hopi scholarship shows the same refusal — if so, the split itself may be the culturally specific item, not the integration.

Three concepts extracted with the authenticity dispute properly attached to all Hatata-derived claims.

Could not find accessible peer-reviewed treatment of Täwaḥǝdo theological love (Flag 3). Did not reach the Ethiopian oral tradition (proverbs, qǝne poetry) that Sumner’s other volumes cover.

**What the reviewer should know:** Watch for two failure modes in this extraction: (1) over-crediting the “love above sectarianism” formulation, which comes from the translation’s framing apparatus and needs verification against the text itself; (2) the entire tradition entering the matrix on contested textual ground — if the d’Urbino thesis were ever vindicated, the “Ethiopian” cell would partly become a “19th-c. European-Ethiopian hybrid” cell. The matrix should be robust to that possibility, which argues for Grok’s run prioritizing non-Hatata Ethiopian sources as a hedge.
